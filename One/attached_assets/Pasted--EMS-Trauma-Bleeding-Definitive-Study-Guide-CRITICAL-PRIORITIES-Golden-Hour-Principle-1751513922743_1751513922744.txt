## ü©∏ BLEEDING CONTROL HIERARCHY

### **External Bleeding Control Methods (In Order)**
1. **Direct Pressure** (Most effective - 5+ minutes)
2. **Pressure Dressing** (Maintain distal pulse)
3. **Tourniquet** (Above bleeding level - extremities only)
4. **Hemostatic Dressing/Wound Packing** (Large wounds)
5. **Junctional Tourniquets** (Groin/axilla)
6. **Air Splints** (Entire extremity injuries)

### **Bleeding Types & Characteristics**
| Type | Color | Flow Pattern | Clotting | Control Difficulty |
|------|-------|--------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Arterial** | Bright Red | Spurts with pulse | Poor | Difficult |
| **Venous** | Dark Red | Steady flow | Good | Easy |
| **Capillary** | Dark Red | Slow ooze | Good | Easy |

## üöó TRAUMA MECHANISMS & INJURY PATTERNS

### **Motor Vehicle Crashes (3 Collisions)**
1. **Vehicle vs Object** ‚Üí Vehicle damage assessment
2. **Passenger vs Interior** ‚Üí Fractures, head trauma
3. **Organs vs Body Structures** ‚Üí Internal injuries (most dangerous)

### **Crash-Specific Injury Patterns**
| Crash Type | Primary Injuries | Key Assessment |
|------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Frontal** | Lower extremity fractures, ribs, head trauma | Restraint systems, airbag deployment |
| **Rear-End** | Whiplash, brain acceleration injury | Headrest position, lap belt injuries |
| **Lateral** | Chest/abdominal, pelvis, ribs | Most lethal - intrusion assessment |
| **Rollover** | Ejection risk, severe multi-trauma | Restraint use critical |
| **Rotational** | Spin-related impacts | Secondary collision risks |

### **High-Risk MOI Indicators**
- Death of occupant
- Severe vehicle deformity/intrusion
- Ejection from vehicle
- Falls >20 feet
- Pedestrian thrown/dragged

## üéØ PENETRATING TRAUMA

### **Velocity Classifications**
| Velocity | Mechanism | Damage Pattern |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Low** | Knives, ice picks | Sharp edge damage along path |
| **Medium/High** | Bullets | Trajectory, fragmentation, cavitation |

### **Cavitation Types**
- **Temporary**: Tissue stretching from pressure
- **Permanent**: Lasting damage along bullet path
- **Exit wounds**: Often larger than entrance

## üí• BLAST INJURIES (4 Mechanisms)

1. **Primary**: Pressure wave ‚Üí Ear/lung/GI damage
2. **Secondary**: Flying debris ‚Üí Penetrating wounds
3. **Tertiary**: Body thrown ‚Üí Fractures, head trauma  
4. **Quaternary**: Burns, toxic gases, crush injuries

**Most Vulnerable**: Air-containing organs (ears, lungs, GI tract)

---

## üîç INTERNAL BLEEDING RECOGNITION

### **Causes**
**Traumatic**: Liver laceration, spleen rupture, pelvic fractures
**Non-Traumatic**: Ulcers, aneurysms, ectopic pregnancy

### **Signs & Symptoms**
**Early Signs**:
- Pain (most common)
- Swelling/distension
- Hematoma/bruising
- Bleeding from body openings

**Late Signs (Shock)**:
- Tachycardia
- Weak pulse
- Cool, clammy skin
- Altered mental status
- Decreased BP

### **Body Opening Bleeding Indicators**
- **Hematemesis**: Vomiting blood
- **Melena**: Black, tarry stool
- **Hematuria**: Blood in urine
- **Hemoptysis**: Coughing blood

## üß† BODY REGION INJURY PATTERNS

### **Head Injuries**
- Frequent neuro exams
- Hidden brain injury risk
- Bleeding/swelling = life-threatening

### **Neck/Throat**
- Airway compromise risk
- DCAP-BTLS assessment
- JVD, tracheal deviation
- Cartilage fracture potential

### **Chest Injuries**
- Broken ribs ‚Üí breathing compromise
- Heart bruising ‚Üí arrhythmias
- Vessel tears ‚Üí massive bleeding
- Pneumothorax/hemothorax

### **Abdominal Injuries**
- **Solid organs** (liver, spleen) ‚Üí bleeding
- **Hollow organs** (stomach, intestines) ‚Üí infection
- High blood flow = rapid deterioration

---

## ‚ö° SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

### **Hemophilia Patients**
- Lack clotting factors
- All injuries potentially serious
- Immediate transport required
- Spontaneous bleeding possible

### **Tourniquet Application**
- **Above** bleeding level
- **Avoid** joints
- **Extremity** bleeding only
- **Never** for closed injuries
- **Document** application time

### **Epistaxis (Nosebleeds)**
- Pinch nostrils for septal bleeding
- Head injury bleeding = skull fracture risk
- Don't apply excessive pressure
- Forward positioning prevents aspiration

## ‚è∞ CRITICAL TIME INTERVALS

- **Scene time**: <10 minutes critical patients
- **Pressure application**: Minimum 5 minutes
- **Reassessment**: Every 5 min (unstable), 15 min (stable)
- **Golden hour**: Definitive care window
- **Clotting time**: ~10 minutes normal response

---

## üö® CONTRAINDICATIONS & WARNINGS

### **Never Do**
- Remove impaled objects
- Use tourniquets on closed injuries
- Apply excessive pressure to head injury bleeding
- Delay transport for complete assessment
- Remove successful pressure dressings

### **Always Remember**
- Standard precautions (PPE)
- Maintain body temperature
- Continuous monitoring
- Document everything
- Communicate early and often

---

## üí° CLINICAL PEARLS

1. **Energy cannot be destroyed, only transferred** - always assess for energy transfer patterns
2. **20% blood loss** = body's tolerance limit (~2 pints)
3. **Rapid bleeding** can cause shock even with <20% loss
4. **Internal bleeding** often more dangerous than obvious external
5. **Capillary refill >2 seconds** in children = significant finding
6. **Target/halo stain** on dressing = possible CSF leak
7. **Rigid, distended abdomen** = internal bleeding until proven otherwise

---

*Remember: When in doubt, treat for the worst-case scenario. It's better to over-treat than under-treat in trauma situations.*
